# soul.md — the mission behind EZ Esports

This file is not marketing copy and it is not a style guide. It is the thing every
other decision in this repo should trace back to: what EZ Esports is for, who it
serves, and what it refuses to become. When a design, a feature, or a line of copy
is in question, the test is not "does this look good" — it's "does this serve the
mission below." If it doesn't, it doesn't belong on the site, no matter how polished.

## Where this came from

EZ Esports started in November 2021 as a group chat between NYC high school esports
club officers who had nowhere organized to play. No league, no schedule, no
structure — just students who wanted to compete building the thing they wished
existed. That origin matters: this was never handed down by adults running a
business. It was built by kids, for kids, out of a real gap.

## The pivot

EZ Esports is transitioning to a nonprofit. That is not a legal technicality — it is
a statement about what this organization optimizes for. A for-profit esports org
optimizes for audience, sponsorship, and spectacle: bigger broadcasts, more
watch-time, a roster of star players who carry the brand. None of that is our job.

Our job is the kid who has never played in an organized match before. The school
with no budget and no equipment. The student who finds a coach, a team, and a
reason to show up through a game their school never funded. A nonprofit answers to
its mission, not to engagement metrics — and our mission is youth development
through esports, full stop.

## Mission

**Give every NYC high schooler organized, coached, competitive esports —
regardless of their school's budget, their skill level, or whether their school
has ever heard of esports before.**

Esports is the hook. Community, skill-building, and a pathway into games and
technology as a career are the point.

## Who we serve

- The student who plays casually and just wants a real team and a real season —
  not just the top 1% who already have a shot at going pro.
- The school with no esports budget, no dedicated space, and no idea where to
  start — not just the schools that already have a gaming lab.
- The kid discovering that shoutcasting, production, or team management might be
  their thing, not just the kid at the keyboard.

If a decision only makes sense for the strongest teams, the best-funded schools, or
the most engaged superfans, it's optimizing for the wrong audience. Design for the
student walking in for the first time.

## Values

**Access.** Free to join. No equipment required to start. Varsity and JV divisions
so skill level is never the gate. If something we build only works for a school
that already has infrastructure, it works against the mission.

**Community.** The relationships between students, schools, and coaches outnumber
the trophies. A season is a success if kids who didn't know each other in
September are a team by June — the standings are a side effect, not the headline.

**Growth.** Esports is the entry point, not the ceiling. Casting, broadcast
production, coaching, event operations, league administration — every one of those
is a skill a student can walk away with. The league should make those paths
visible, not just the player pathway.

**Belonging.** Every school, from the ones with a dedicated gaming lab to the ones
running matches on borrowed laptops, gets the same schedule, the same standings
page, and the same shot at the finals.

## What we are not

- We are not a broadcast company chasing viewership. Livestreams exist to include
  families and friends who can't be in the room, not to build a media brand.
- We are not a talent pipeline that only cares about the students good enough to
  go pro. We measure success in participation and growth, not in draft picks.
- We are not gatekept. No tryout should exist to keep a kid out — divisions exist
  to make sure everyone is playing a game they can actually win.
- We are not a business that happens to serve students. We are a youth
  organization that happens to use esports as the tool.

## What this means for the site

The homepage's job is not to look like a professional esports org's — it's to make
a student, a parent, or a school's first impression be: *this is organized, this is
for me, and I can start today.* Data and scores prove the league is real and
active; community and access prove it's for them. Neither wins the page alone —
scoreboard-only reads as elite and competitive-first, marketing-only reads as
hollow. The two have to sit together, with community and access leading.

Visually: EZ pink (`#f4cccc`) is not a decoration on top of black — it's the
warmth against the black's intensity. Pure black-and-white reads corporate and
competitive; pure pink reads soft. The blend is the point, because that's the
actual tension in the mission — competitive esports *and* youth accessibility, held
at once, neither winning.
